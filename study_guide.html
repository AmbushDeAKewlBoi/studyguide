CIA Triangle — Confidentiality, Integrity & Availability

Objectives 1.1.a · 1.1.b · 1.1.c · 1.1.d

The CIA Triangle is the foundational policy model for organizational data security. Every security decision maps back to one of its three pillars.

Confidentiality
Keeping systems and data private from unauthorized users. Achieved through authentication (verifying identity) and encryption (scrambling data so only authorized parties can read it). Files marked "Internal Only," "Confidential," or "For Authorized Users" fall under this pillar. All hardware and software must also be kept secure.
Integrity
Ensuring data is accurate, valid, and protected against unauthorized changes — whether stored or in transit. Secured through hashing (which detects tampering), checksums, digital signatures, and access controls that prevent unauthorized writes. Data must not be altered en route from sender to receiver.
Availability
Keeping data accessible to authorized users when they need it — but only then. Protected by redundancy, failover systems, DDoS mitigation, and strong uptime management. The three main threats to availability are: hardware failure, software failure, and network/service denial attacks.
Non-Repudiation
The ability to prove that a specific entity performed a specific action — preventing them from denying it later. Achieved through digital signatures, audit logs, and timestamped records. Critical for accountability and legal/compliance purposes.
Exam Focus

Strong authentication systems combine multiple layers: username/password (what you know) + token (what you have) + biometrics (who you are). Permissions protect file types by restricting read/write access. Encryption protects data at rest and in transit. A vulnerability is defined as any weakness in the CIA of data.

Threats, Risk, and the Risk Register

Objectives 1.1.e · 1.1.f · 1.1.g

Threats are weaknesses in infrastructure. A risk is the probability that a threat will become an actual incident. A vulnerability is the specific weakness being exploited. A Risk Register is a tracking tool to assess and manage all identified risks.

Risk Acceptance
Acknowledging that a risk exists but taking no action. You document it and accept the potential consequence. Also called "do nothing."
Risk Avoidance
Eliminating the risk by not engaging in the risky activity at all (e.g., not using public Wi-Fi for sensitive transfers).
Risk Mitigation
Lessening the impact or probability of a risk through controls (firewalls, antivirus, patches). Most common strategy. Leaves residual risk.
Risk Transfer
Shifting the risk burden to another party — typically through cyber insurance or outsourcing.

A Risk Register tracks: risk categories, risk probability (low/medium/high), risk impact (low/medium/high), threshold scores, and contingency plans. A contingency plan is a pre-planned response to a risk that becomes an incident — it defines exactly what to do when things go wrong.

Key Formula

Risk = Threat × Vulnerability. To find a threshold, multiply probability by impact. Risks above the threshold require a contingency plan and active management.

Other Core Security Principles

Objectives 1.1.h · 1.1.i · 1.1.s
Principle of Least Privilege
Users, systems, and processes receive only the minimum permissions needed to perform their specific job — nothing more. Applies to systems, resources, and physical access. Enforced through file/folder permission assignments and group-based access control. Reducing unnecessary privileges directly reduces the organization's attack surface.
Social Engineering
Attempting to gain sensitive information by tricking users into providing it. Examples: phishing emails, vishing (voice calls), impersonating IT help desk, baiting (leaving USB drives). Sensitive information should NEVER be divulged to someone attempting to obtain it. Mitigation: user training, strict verification procedures, skepticism of unsolicited contact.
Attack Surface
The total sum of all ways a system can be attacked. Increases with: more installed apps, more running services, more connected peripherals, more network devices, more users, IoT devices. Decreases with: uninstalling unused apps, disabling unused services, disconnecting unneeded peripherals, applying the Principle of Least Privilege.
Threat Modeling
The systematic process of identifying threats and vulnerabilities, then defining countermeasures to prevent them. Involves: identifying assets, identifying threats, analyzing attack surfaces, rating risks, and planning mitigations. STRIDE is a popular threat modeling framework (Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service, Elevation of Privilege).

Security Policy Types — Administrative & Technical Controls

Objectives 1.3.a · 1.3.b

Security controls usually fall into one of two categories: administrative or technical. Together they form the backbone of an organization's security posture.

Administrative Controls

Controls that work through policies, procedures, and guidelines. These are management-oriented rules that dictate how employees should behave and how the organization handles security.

  • Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Defines what employees can and cannot do with company resources (internet, email, devices)
  • Security awareness training: Educating employees about phishing, social engineering, and safe computing practices
  • Incident response plans: Documented procedures for responding to security breaches
  • Change management: Formal process for making changes to IT systems
  • Background checks: Screening employees before granting access to sensitive systems
Technical Controls

Also known as technological controls, these control network security through hardware and software mechanisms.

  • Firewalls: Control traffic by limiting ports and protocols allowed for inbound and outbound traffic
  • Antivirus/Antimalware: Software that detects and removes malicious code
  • Encryption: Scrambles data so only authorized parties can read it
  • IDS/IPS: Monitors and/or blocks suspicious network activity
  • Access control lists (ACLs): Rules defining who can access what resources
  • Authentication systems: MFA, smart cards, biometrics
Exam Tip

Administrative controls are people-focused (policies, training, procedures). Technical controls are technology-focused (firewalls, encryption, IDS). Both are necessary — technical controls without administrative policies leave gaps in human behavior, and policies without technical enforcement are just suggestions.

Physical Security

Objectives 1.2.a · 1.2.b · 1.2.c · 1.2.d
Site Security — Defense in Depth

Physical security is layered — called Defense in Depth. Three areas create site security:

  • External perimeter: The entry area and immediate outside of a building. Secured by: fences, bollards, lighting, security cameras, badge readers at entry gates, security guards.
  • Internal perimeter: The area immediately inside the entryway. Secured by: locked interior doors, reception desk, visitor badges, alarm systems.
  • Secure internal areas: Server rooms, data centers, executive offices. Secured by: key-card access, biometric scanners, mantraps, security cameras, equipment cages.
Computer Security & Removable Drives
Physical Computer Security
Cable locks secure computers and servers to desks/racks to prevent theft. Servers without a dedicated server room should be locked in a secure cabinet or closet with restricted key access.
Removable Drive Security
Removable devices include: USB drives, DVDs, external hard drives, removable memory cards. Encrypt them with BitLocker To Go. If removable drives aren't needed, disable/lock the ports. To prevent malicious data from entering your network via removable media, disable AutoRun/AutoPlay.
BitLocker
Encrypts entire internal drives on Windows. Requires a TPM chip or startup key. Protects data if a drive is removed from the machine.
BitLocker To Go
Encrypts removable drives (USB drives, external HDDs). The drive can be used on other Windows machines after entering the recovery key or password.
Access Control, Device Security & Mantraps

Access control restricts both physical equipment access and file/server access to only those with permission.

Mobile device security: Personal phones used for work data should be password-protected and/or encrypted. Mobile Device Management (MDM) software can remotely wipe devices, enforce encryption, control app installation, and track device location.

What is a Mantrap?
A physical security gateway with two interlocked doors — only one can open at a time. Purpose: to capture individuals who enter a building illegally, especially through tailgating (following a credentialed person in without their own credentials). A person caught in a mantrap is held until security personnel arrive. Mantraps are common in data centers, government buildings, and high-security corporate facilities.
Keyloggers & Physical Security

Wireless keyboards increase the risk of keylogger attacks since signals can be intercepted. A hardware keylogger is a physical device plugged between keyboard and computer — antivirus cannot detect it. A software keylogger can be detected by antivirus. Keyloggers can capture credentials, enabling attackers to access network resources.

Malware Types

Objectives 1.4.a through 1.4.k, 1.4.n–1.4.u
Buffer Overflow

A buffer overflow occurs when a program writes more data to a buffer (reserved memory space) than it can hold. The overflow spills into adjacent memory, potentially overwriting data or injecting malicious code. This leaves the application susceptible to exploitation — attackers can execute arbitrary code with the application's privileges. Root cause: lack of bounds checking in code.

Viruses, Worms & Trojan Horses
Virus
Malicious code that must attach to a carrier (a file, program, document) before it can spread and cause damage. Requires user action to execute (opening an infected file). Types include: file infectors, macro viruses, boot sector viruses.
Polymorphic Virus
A virus that constantly changes its signature code (mutates) to evade antivirus detection. Also called a metamorphic virus. A virus hoax is a fake virus that looks real but isn't — used to distract from real threats or cause panic.
Worm
Self-replicating malicious code that spreads without needing a carrier or host. Worms don't usually destroy data but consume system resources: CPU, bandwidth, and memory. They exploit network vulnerabilities to propagate. Example: WannaCry.
Trojan Horse
An imposter program that appears legitimate and useful but contains hidden malicious functionality. Unlike viruses, Trojans don't self-replicate. They rely on users installing them voluntarily. Examples: fake antivirus software, cracked software downloads. Once installed, they may open backdoors, steal data, or download additional malware.
Spyware, Ransomware & Adware
Spyware
Collects personal information without the user's knowledge. Usually installed secretly. Sends captured data to third-party advertisers or attackers. Different from cookies: cookies are legitimate text files storing site preferences; spyware is malicious and operates covertly.
Ransomware — Two Types
Locker ransomware: Locks the user completely out of their device/OS. Crypto ransomware: Encrypts specific files, preventing access. Both demand payment (ransom) for restoration. Notable examples: CryptoLocker, WannaCry, Petya.
Adware
Displays unwanted advertisements (pop-ups) within programs. Often downloaded without user consent bundled with free software. Adware is not always malicious but degrades user experience and may track browsing behavior.
Rootkits, Backdoors & Zero Day Attacks
Rootkit
Malware that gains administrative/root-level access and masks itself to avoid detection. Often affects BIOS/firmware or kernel-level code. Hard to detect because it operates at the OS security level. Can disable antivirus software from within the OS. Removal often requires a clean OS reinstall.
Backdoor
Malware that accesses a system by exploiting security or code weaknesses. Can also be a deliberately left debug/access code that was never removed from production software. Provides persistent unauthorized remote access. Example: a developer leaving a hardcoded password in a production app.
Zero Day Attack
An attack that exploits a previously unknown vulnerability — the vendor has had zero days to create a patch. The vulnerability is exploited the moment the attacker discovers it, before the developer can release a fix. Extremely dangerous because no defenses exist yet. Patching immediately when vendors release updates is the primary defense.
Exam Critical — Malware Identification

Know which malware needs a carrier (virus) vs. which is self-replicating (worm). Know that rootkits hide at OS level. Backdoors use existing weaknesses OR leftover developer code. Zero Day: unknown until attacked.

Denial-of-Service (DoS) Attacks

Objective 1.4.l

A DoS (Denial of Service) attack floods a network, device, or service with useless data packets until it can no longer function. The goal is to disrupt — not necessarily to steal data.

DoS
Single machine sends flood of packets to a target. Easier to block — just block that IP address.
DDoS (Distributed DoS)
Multiple machines (often thousands) simultaneously attack a target. Much harder to block. Orchestrated using a botnet.
Botnet
A network of computers that have been taken over (compromised) to carry out a DDoS attack. The attacker controls them remotely via command-and-control (C2) servers.
Zombie
An individual computer that has been infected and is now part of a botnet, carrying out DDoS attacks without the owner's knowledge. Also called a "bot."
Email Bombing
A DoS attack targeting email — sending massive volumes of email to a single address to overwhelm the mail server and create a denial of service for that address.
Best Defense
Firewalls and network-layer filtering devices (routers with ACLs, DDoS mitigation appliances, IPS systems) are best suited for warding off DoS attacks. Cloud-based DDoS scrubbing services can absorb volumetric attacks.

Vulnerability Types — XSS, SQL Injection & Input Validation

Objectives 1.4.n · 1.4.o · 1.4.p

Vulnerabilities are potential weaknesses in a network security infrastructure. Web application vulnerabilities are especially dangerous because they expose data to anyone on the internet.

Software Vulnerability Attack
An attack in which a virus or worm takes advantage of a software vulnerability — an unpatched bug or flaw in code. These attacks exploit the gap between when a vulnerability is discovered and when a patch is applied. Keeping software updated is the primary defense.
Cross-Site Scripting (XSS) — Deep Dive
An injection of a malicious script that bypasses a web browser's security mechanism. The attacker targets client-side scripts that run in the user's browser. XSS exploits the user's trust in a website — the malicious script appears to come from the trusted site. Two main types: Stored XSS (script saved on server, affects all visitors) and Reflected XSS (script embedded in a URL, affects users who click it). Prevention: input validation and output encoding — ensure form fields do not accept text that looks like scripting code.
SQL Injection — Deep Dive
An attack in which SQL code is used to gain access to a SQL database source and then run malicious code. With a request form whose fields can accept any entry, an attacker can type SQL statements to either get or change information in a database. Prevention: use parameterized queries (prepared statements), validate all input fields, and never allow raw SQL in user input fields. Code used to build a webpage must be validated so form fields do not allow text that could infiltrate an app.
Input Validation
The process of ensuring that form fields and input areas only accept expected data types and formats. Without input validation, forms can accept scripting code (enabling XSS) or SQL statements (enabling SQL injection). All user input should be treated as potentially malicious. Validation should occur both client-side (for user experience) and server-side (for security — client-side can be bypassed).
Exam Critical — Vulnerability vs. Attack

A vulnerability is a weakness (e.g., unvalidated form fields). An attack exploits that weakness (e.g., SQL injection through those fields). The exam tests your ability to differentiate: XSS targets client-side scripts (browser-side), while SQL injection targets server-side databases. Know both the vulnerability and the mitigation for each.

Brute Force, MITM & MITB Attacks

Objectives 1.4.q · 1.4.r
Brute Force Attack
An attacker (with the help of an automated tool) systematically tries every possible password combination until the correct one is found. Mitigation: strong password policies (complexity requirements, minimum length), account lockout after N failed attempts, multi-factor authentication. Dictionary attacks are a variant — they use lists of common words/passwords rather than every possible combination.
Man-in-the-Middle (MITM)
An attack where the attacker positions themselves between two communicating parties, secretly intercepting and potentially altering the data in transit. Neither party knows the attacker is present. The attacker can read sensitive data, modify transactions, or inject malicious content. Prevention: use end-to-end encryption (TLS/HTTPS), certificate pinning, VPNs, and avoid public Wi-Fi for sensitive transactions.
Man-in-the-Browser (MITB)
A specific type of MITM attack where malware infects a web browser. The attacker sends a script to a website and obtains privileges that allow them to: inject code, change form data (e.g., bank transfer amounts), set up proxies, and inspect cookies and session tokens. Happens transparently — the user sees the correct webpage while the attacker manipulates the underlying transaction. Prevention: browser integrity checks, anti-malware, transaction authentication numbers (TANs).

Keyloggers & Logic Bombs

Objectives 1.4.t · 1.4.u
Software Keylogger
A program that records keystrokes. Detectable by antivirus software. Often installed by Trojans or malware. Example use: capturing usernames, passwords, credit card numbers, PINs.
Hardware Keylogger
A physical device plugged between keyboard and USB/PS2 port. NOT detectable by antivirus. Only detectable by physical inspection. Example: a tiny dongle between the keyboard cable and computer port.
Screen Capture / Keystroke via Camera
Keystrokes can also be captured through video recording (shoulder surfing, covert cameras), acoustic analysis, or electromagnetic eavesdropping — not pure keylogging but similar result.
Logic Bomb
Malicious code that lies dormant until a specific trigger event activates it — either a specific date/time or a specific event (e.g., an employee being fired). Classic example: a disgruntled employee sets a logic bomb to delete company files two weeks after they leave. Administrators must stay updated on any known logic bomb speculations scheduled to detonate.

Backup & Restore Types

Objectives 1.5.a · 1.5.b · 1.5.c
Full Backup
  • Backs up all selected files
  • Resets the archive bit (marks files as backed up)
  • Slowest to perform, most storage
  • Usually done weekly, not daily
  • Fastest restoration — only one set needed
Incremental Backup
  • Backs up all files changed since last backup of any type
  • Smallest backup size per session
  • Slowest restoration — need last full + ALL incrementals since
  • Good for daily use between full backups
Differential Backup
  • Backs up all files changed since the last full backup
  • Grows larger each time until next full backup
  • Restoration: last full + last differential only
  • Middle ground between full and incremental
Restoration Scenario — Exam Favorite

Schedule: Sun (Full), Mon (Differential), Tue (Incremental), Wed (Differential), Thu (Incremental), Fri (Differential). If the system crashes Wednesday morning: you need Sunday's Full backup + Tuesday's Incremental backup (most recent before the crash). Note: this scenario uses both types — carefully read what schedule is given.

Part of a good backup strategy: ensure backups (especially full backups) do not interfere with normal day-to-day operations — schedule them during off-hours. Also encrypt backup media and store copies offsite.

Domain 2

Operating System Security

Client/server protection, hardening, authentication, permissions, auditing, and the full spectrum of encryption — from EFS to PKI.

Client & Server Protection

Objectives 2.1.a through 2.1.j
Separation of Services & Hardening
Separation of Services
Different server roles should run on different physical or virtual servers. Never install AD, DNS, and routing on one server. Benefit: lowers the attack surface of each server; if one is compromised, others remain safe.
Hardening
Reducing a device's attack surface by: removing unneeded accounts, disabling unnecessary services, using firewalls to isolate servers, avoiding default administrator accounts (rename them), applying all patches. A domain controller should only run services related to running a domain.
Patch Management
Ensuring devices and apps always have the latest patches (security updates). Devices should be set to receive or automatically download patches. Tools: WSUS (Windows Server Update Services), SCCM (System Center Configuration Manager). The attack surface of a device equals the sum of all potential vulnerabilities on it.
Reducing Attack Surfaces
Uninstall unused apps, disable unused services, unplug unneeded peripherals. Every installed app, running service, and connected device increases your attack surface.
Group Policy

Group Policy controls rights, settings, and configurations for users, groups, and OUs across a Windows domain. Updates push automatically every ~90 minutes by default.

CommandFunction
gpupdate /forceForces immediate Group Policy refresh on a device
gpresult /RShows the Resultant Set of Policies (RSoP) applied to a device — domain policy takes precedence over local policy
DNS & Authentication Protocol Security
Secure Dynamic DNS Updates
Only Active Directory domain members can create DNS records. Prevents DNS poisoning by unauthorized record additions. Set via right-clicking domain in DNS Manager → Properties → allowing only Secure dynamic updates. The "None" option prevents all dynamic updates (manual only).
Unsecure Auth Protocols
FTP, Telnet, and PAP transmit data (including credentials) in plain text — never use on secure servers. Replace Telnet with SSH (encrypted). Replace FTP with SFTP or FTPS. SFTP runs on port 22. The problem: these protocols are unsecure because they don't encrypt authentication traffic.
Read-Only Domain Controllers (RODC)
A domain controller that holds a read-only copy of Active Directory. Changes cannot be made at the local level — they must come from a writable DC pushing down to the RODC. Ideal for: remote/branch offices where physical security of the DC cannot be guaranteed. Gets its database from a writable domain controller.
UAC (User Account Control)
Windows feature that prompts for confirmation or administrator credentials when software tries to make system changes. Protects against unauthorized installations. Has 4 settings (off, notify only for app changes (recommended), always notify, most restrictive). Default = notify only when apps try to make changes.
Software Restriction Policies & Antivirus

AppLocker provides more granular software restriction than the older Software Restriction Policies tool. Rules can be enforced for: executable files, Windows Installer files, scripts, DLL files, and packaged apps. You can allow rules or audit what users are doing.

Antivirus (Windows Defender): Virus definitions must be constantly updated. The History tab shows quarantined items. If a machine may be infected, first action: disconnect it from the network to prevent spread, then run a full scan. A fake virus is called a hoax.

Offline Files: Allow working on network files while disconnected. Available via Sync Center in Control Panel. Changes sync when reconnected. Always encrypt offline files for security. Available only when connected to a Windows domain network.

User Authentication

Objectives 2.2.a through 2.2.e
Multifactor Authentication (MFA)
What You Know
  • Password
  • PIN
  • Security questions
  • Passphrase

Most common factor. Weakest alone.

What You Have
  • Smart card
  • Token device (RSA key fob)
  • Phone (receives OTP)
  • Hardware key (YubiKey)

Physical object — lost = locked out.

Who You Are
  • Fingerprint scanner
  • Retina/iris scan
  • Voice recognition
  • Facial recognition

Biometrics — most secure, hardest to fake.

Critical MFA Rule

Username + Password = single factor (both are "what you know"). True MFA requires factors from different categories. Not all users need to be authenticated the same way — privileged accounts need stronger authentication.

Password Policy

A strong password has at least 8 characters and at least 3 of the 4 character types: uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols. Microsoft's default minimum is 0 (no minimum) — administrators must set it.

PolicySettingWhy
Minimum Length≥ 8 characters (ideally 12+)Longer passwords exponentially increase brute force time

Password HistoryRemember last 10–24 passwordsPrevents reuse of old compromised passwords
Max Password Age90 days recommendedLimits exposure window if password is compromised
Min Password Age1 dayPrevents users from cycling through history to reuse favorites
Account LockoutLock after 3–5 failed attemptsPrevents brute force attacks

Location: Local Security Policy (individual machines) → Account Policies → Password Policy. Or domain-level Group Policy Object.

Smart Cards, RADIUS & Biometrics
Smart Card
A badge/card containing a chip with authentication data. Swiped, waved, or inserted into a reader. Typically used as one factor of multifactor authentication. Bank debit cards are contact-based smart cards. NOT all smart cards are physical — virtual smart cards exist.
Virtual Smart Card
Uses the TPM (Trusted Platform Module) chip on a computer — not a separate physical card. Provides equivalent security to a physical smart card without the hardware cost. Stored on the computer itself.
RADIUS
Remote Authentication Dial-In User Service. Authenticates external connections from: dial-ups, VPNs, web servers, wireless access points, and VPN concentrators. Most common use: authenticating wireless network access. Not proprietary (unlike Cisco's TACACS+).
Run As / sudo
Windows: Run As allows running a program under a different account (e.g., administrator). Does NOT require being logged in as admin, but requires the admin password. Linux equivalent: sudo (superuser do) — runs commands with elevated privileges after entering the sudo password.
Active Directory

Active Directory (AD) is the central management system for a Windows domain — a large database storing users, groups, computers, printers, and other objects. Uses LDAP (Lightweight Directory Access Protocol) to query and modify data. Default auth protocol: Kerberos. Backup protocol: NTLM. Enables Single Sign-On (SSO) — one login accesses multiple resources.

Key AD structure: Users → Groups → Organizational Units (OUs). OUs allow applying Group Policies to subsets of objects. Security groups have permissions assigned; Distribution groups are email-only.

Permissions — Windows & Linux

Objectives 2.3.a through 2.3.g
File System Permissions (NTFS)

Best practice: assign permissions to groups, then add users to groups. The "Folder" permission is not available for individual files (only available for folders). Implicit deny: if a permission is not explicitly granted, it is denied by default.

PermissionWhat It Allows

Read & Execute (RX)All Read permissions + run executable files.
List Folder ContentsView names of files/folders inside a folder. Folders only — not available for files.

Modify (M)Read, write, execute, and delete files/folders. Cannot change permissions or take ownership.
Full Control (FC)All Modify permissions + change permissions + take ownership. Most powerful.
Default Permissions Rule

When permissions are first granted to a user or group, the defaults assigned are: Read & Execute, List Folder Contents, and Read. Use the Deny column sparingly — Deny always overrides Allow.

Permission Inheritance

Permissions flow from parent folders down to child folders/files (inherited). A gray check mark in the permissions dialog indicates an inherited permission. You can break inheritance by converting to explicit permissions. Danger: if you remove inherited permissions and you're not in the parent admin group, you may lose your own access.

Copying vs. Moving Files
ActionSame DiskDifferent Disk
CopyNew file gets permissions of destination folderNew file gets permissions of destination folder
MoveFile keeps original permissionsNew file gets permissions of destination folder

The only instance where permissions move with a file: moving it to another location on the same hard drive/disk.

Multiple Groups & Effective Permissions

When a user belongs to multiple groups with different permissions on a resource, they receive the combined (most permissive) effective permissions from all groups — unless Deny is applied (Deny always wins).

Share Permissions

Share permissions have only three levels: Full Control, Change, Read. Less granular than NTFS permissions. Share permissions do not apply when locally logged in to the machine — only apply over the network. When both share and NTFS permissions exist, the most restrictive applies.

Registry

The Registry is Windows' central database storing all system and application configuration settings. Access via regedit.exe. Very few users should have access. Filled with keys (organized groups of settings) and values (32-bit data representations for a key or subkey).

HKEY_CLASSES_ROOT
Handles file associations (e.g., .docx opens with Word)
HKEY_CURRENT_USER
Settings specific to the currently logged-in user
HKEY_USERS
All users' settings stored here. HKEY_CURRENT_USER is a subset.
HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE
Machine-specific settings (hardware, OS, installed software)
HKEY_CURRENT_CONFIG
Volatile configuration info about the current hardware profile

Volatile hives (lost on reboot): HKEY_CURRENT_USER and HKEY_CURRENT_CONFIG. To control who can make registry changes: Security tab in registry key Properties → Permissions.

Permissions in Linux

Linux has three permission holders: Owner (User), Group, and Others. The "Others" permission encompasses default/global access. Permissions are set using the chmod command or GUI. Closing a terminal in Linux saves permission changes to edited files.

Ownership & Delegation

Every resource has one owner with full control. Ownership can be changed via the Security tab → Advanced → Owner. Delegation allows one or more individuals to take administrative control of an AD Organizational Unit via the Delegation of Control Wizard. Always assign delegation to a group (not just one person) for continuity.

Audit Policies

Objectives 2.4.a through 2.4.f

Auditing tracks who has logged into a system and what has been accessed — enabling non-repudiation. Two auditing types: Success (logs successful events) and Failure (logs failed attempts). Both should be enabled for comprehensive security monitoring.

What Can Be Audited?
Account logon events, account management, directory service access, logon events, object access (files/folders), policy changes, privilege use, process tracking, and system events. For non-Active Directory objects (printers, files without AD context), enable the Object Access audit policy. For non-Microsoft products: use Syslog — an industry-standard audit tool.
How to Enable Auditing
Local level: Local Security Policy → Local Policies → Audit Policy. Domain level: Group Policy (GPO) applied to the domain. Setting up audit parameters alone does NOT enable auditing — you must explicitly enable it.
Where Are Audit Results Stored?
In the Windows Event Viewer → Windows Logs → Security. Log path typically: C:\Windows\System32\winevt\Logs\Security.evtx. Contains date, task category, and resource accessed. To secure audit logs: encrypt the folder containing them (via EFS or BitLocker). Set folder permissions so only authorized personnel can access the audit log folder.

Encryption

Objectives 2.5.a through 2.5.h
EFS — Encrypting File System

EFS encrypts individual files and folders on NTFS volumes. Located under Advanced Attributes in a file's Properties → General tab. If you encrypt a file, you cannot compress the same file. Encrypted files appear green in Windows Explorer.

Encryption and moving/copying rules: If an encrypted file is moved or copied to another NTFS volume, encryption stays on the file. If moved to a non-NTFS volume (FAT32, exFAT), encryption is removed.

Drive Encryption

BitLocker encrypts entire drives. Uses TPM (Trusted Platform Module) chip if available. TPM is hardware-based encryption — stores cryptographic keys in hardware, providing stronger protection than software-only encryption. BitLocker uses AES-128 or AES-256.

Symmetric vs. Asymmetric Encryption
Symmetric Encryption

Same key used to encrypt AND decrypt. Faster. Key distribution is the challenge.

Block ciphers: DES (56-bit, weak), 3DES (triple DES, better), AES (128/192/256-bit — most secure, recommended)

Stream cipher: RC4 (bit-by-bit encryption — faster but weaker)

Asymmetric Encryption

One key encrypts, a different key decrypts. Slower but solves key distribution. Used at start of transmission.

Public key: shared openly, used to encrypt data at the beginning of transmission

Private key: kept secret by the owner, used to decrypt data at the end

Examples: RSA, ECC (used between web servers and browsers — HTTPS)

Hash Functions

One-way encryption with NO decryption key. Used to protect passwords (MD5, SHA-1, SHA-256). Even if the hash is stolen, the original password cannot be recovered. If two inputs produce the same hash (collision), the hash function is compromised.

Mail Encryption
PGP (Pretty Good Privacy)
Encrypts email with a public key and a session key. Recipient uses their private key to extract the session key, then both keys decrypt the message. Used by individuals and open-source communities.
S/MIME
Secure/Multipurpose Internet Mail Extension. Used by most email providers and web browsers. Also used to embed objects in email. Requires certificates from a Certificate Authority.
Digital Signature
Used to verify the authenticity of a document or email — proving it came from who it claims and was not tampered with. Uses the sender's private key to sign; recipient uses sender's public key to verify.
SSL / TLS
SSL (Secure Socket Layer) uses asymmetric + symmetric encryption. Primary use: websites (HTTPS) and VPNs. TLS (Transport Layer Security) is the newer, more secure extension of SSL. Used for HTTPS (port 443). SSL uses both public and private key encryption.

Secure Communication Processes

Objective 2.5.e

Communication channels — email, texting, chat, and social media — all require security consideration. Data shared through these channels can be intercepted, leaked, or exploited if not properly secured.

Email Security
Sensitive information transmitted via email should be encrypted using S/MIME or PGP. Never send passwords, financial data, or personally identifiable information (PII) through unencrypted email. Email servers should use TLS for transport encryption and SPF/DKIM/DMARC records to prevent spoofing.
Social Media Policy
A best practice is to never post any company information to social media without prior approval from whoever runs the company's social media policy. Social media accounts can be compromised and used for social engineering. Employees should be trained on what is and isn't acceptable to share publicly.
Texting & Chat Security
Instant messaging and texting apps used for business should support end-to-end encryption. Sensitive data should never be transmitted through unencrypted chat channels. Business chat platforms (Teams, Slack) should be configured with proper data loss prevention (DLP) policies.
Secure Website Verification
A secure website starts with an HTTPS prefix (not HTTP). The browser displays a lock (padlock) icon next to the URL to indicate the connection is encrypted. Clicking the lock icon shows the website's digital certificate, including the issuing CA, validity period, and encryption details. Always verify the padlock before entering sensitive information.
Exam Focus — Social Media

The exam may ask about social media best practices. Key rule: don't post company information to social media without prior approval from the person managing the company's social media policy. This applies to all employees, not just marketing.

VPN Tunneling & Authentication Protocols

Objective 2.5.f

A VPN (Virtual Private Network) is an encrypted private transmission over a public network, used to secure remote connections. Consists of a tunneling protocol (creates the encrypted tunnel) and an authentication form (verifies the user).

ProtocolTypeKey Details

Split Tunneling

An active VPN defaults to sending all internet traffic through its encrypted tunnel. Split tunneling allows only corporate traffic to go through the VPN while regular internet traffic goes directly to the internet — reducing VPN load but potentially less secure for internet traffic.

Network Device Security

Wireless security standards, firewall types, intrusion detection, network isolation techniques, and protocol security concepts.

Wireless Security

Objectives 3.1.a · 3.1.b · 3.1.c
WEP (Avoid)
Wired Equivalent Privacy. Oldest wireless encryption standard. Has been cracked — should NEVER be used. Uses RC4 stream cipher with weak key management. If you see WEP on an exam question about what to avoid, that's your answer.
WPA (Legacy)
Wi-Fi Protected Access. Uses TKIP (Temporal Key Integrity Protocol) as a stopgap improvement over WEP. Better than WEP but still outdated. Uses a shared key to secure wireless networks. Some older networks still use it.
WPA2 (Use This)
Successor to WPA. Uses AES encryption and IEEE 802.11i standard. Two modes: WPA2-Personal (shared key) and WPA2-Enterprise (RADIUS authentication). CCMP protocol. Most secure of the three. WPA3 is even newer.
SSIDs, MAC Filtering & OOBE
SSID
Service Set Identifier — the name of a wireless network. Disabling SSID broadcast hides the network name (security by obscurity — not truly secure, but adds a layer). In high-security environments with SSID broadcast off, you need: the SSID name AND the password to connect.
MAC Filtering
Controls which devices can connect by their MAC (hardware) address. Only listed MAC addresses can reach the WAP. Limitation: impractical for networks with more than 10 devices. MAC addresses can be spoofed (cloned) — not fully secure. Works best for very small, controlled networks.
OOBE (Out-of-Box Experience)
Default factory configuration of a wireless access point. Default credentials (admin/admin, admin/password) are easily found online. Always change: default admin username & password, default SSID name, and default Wi-Fi password immediately upon setup. Access configuration via the Basic tab of the WAP admin interface.

Firewalls

Objectives 3.2.a through 3.2.e

A firewall controls port and protocol traffic entering/exiting a network based on administrator-defined rules. Can create three network zones: public (internet), perimeter (DMZ), and private (internal).

GenerationLayer (OSI)TypeHow It Works
1st GenerationLayer 3 (Network)Packet FilteringFilters packets by IP address, port, protocol. Stateless. Simple and fast but limited.
2nd GenerationLayer 5 (Session)Circuit FilteringAnalyzes entire sessions (connections), not just individual packets. More context-aware.
3rd GenerationLayer 7 (Application)Application-Level (Proxy)Deep packet inspection at application layer. Can inspect content. Slowest but most thorough. Proxy server is an example.
Hardware Firewalls
Physical devices with their own operating system. Dedicated to firewall function. Scales well for large networks. Best for enterprise/large network infrastructure. Examples: Cisco ASA, Palo Alto Networks, Fortinet.
Software Firewalls
Apps running on existing hardware. Consume system resources (CPU, RAM). One system per instance. Good for home/small office when hardware firewall isn't cost-effective. Example: Windows Defender Firewall with Advanced Security (a host-based firewall).
Network Firewall
Protects the edge of a network or inner parts from unwanted traffic. Best for large network infrastructure. Positioned at network perimeter. Controls all traffic entering/leaving the entire network.
Host Firewall
Protects inbound/outbound traffic on a single host. Best for small networks or individual devices. Windows Defender Firewall is an example. Only enables features actually needed.
Stateful vs. Stateless Inspection
Stateful Firewall
Monitors and stores the state of active connections. Allows return traffic from established outbound connections even if all inbound is blocked. Example: if you visit a website, the response packets are allowed back because the firewall knows you initiated the connection. More secure and intelligent.
Stateless Firewall
Only examines individual packets in isolation — no connection context. Inspects: source IP, destination IP, packet type, port number. Faster but less intelligent. Cannot distinguish legitimate return traffic from an attack. Used in high-speed environments where performance matters more than granular control.

Security Compliance Manager (SCM): Free Microsoft tool for establishing security baselines according to industry standards. Used for security baseline management. Defense in Depth: the concept of using multiple overlapping layers of security — so if one fails, others remain. Security Baselines: a grouping of industry-standard settings that provide a positive security impact.

IDS, IPS & SIEM

Objectives 3.2.f · 3.2.g · 3.2.h
IDS — Intrusion Detection System
Monitors network traffic and alerts when suspicious activity is detected. Passive — it detects and reports but does not stop the attack automatically. Uses signature files (baselines of normal activity) to identify anomalies.
IPS — Intrusion Prevention System
Monitors traffic AND actively blocks or shuts down suspicious activity. Active — it detects AND prevents. Can automatically shut down ports, block IP addresses, or terminate connections. Essentially an IDS with automated response capability.
SIEM — Security Information & Event Manager
Collects security data from multiple sources, creates security reports, and automates tasks. Provides real-time analysis of security alerts. Example: Azure Sentinel (cloud-based). Combines SIEM with SOAR (Security Orchestration, Automation, Response).
Content Filtering, Blacklisting & Whitelisting
Content filtering keeps unwanted information out of sight — filtering webpages, email content, files. In Edge/IE: SmartScreen filter for phishing, Family Safety area for content filtering. Blacklisting: maintaining a list of banned/blocked items (blocked executables, blocked URLs). Whitelisting: maintaining a list of approved/allowed items — more restrictive, only listed items are permitted. If the default security level is set to Disallowed, the allowed-executables list becomes a whitelist.

Network Isolation Methods

Objectives 3.3.a through 3.3.i
Routing

Routers connect network segments and use routing tables to determine packet destinations. Most routers are classified as hardware routers. Software routers: Windows Server, Azure routing tables. Two ways to build a routing table: static (manually defined) and dynamic (automatically built).

Distance-Vector Routing
Makes routing decisions based on hop count (number of routers to destination). Simpler but less optimal. Protocols: RIP.
Link-State Routing
Considers multiple factors: hop count, distance, speed, and bandwidth. More accurate but uses more processing power. Protocols: OSPF, IS-IS.
Static Routing
Manually defined routes. More secure (no dynamic updates to exploit). Recommended for sensitive communications (static routes don't broadcast routing info). No automatic failover.
Split Horizon
A loop-prevention technique that prevents a router from advertising a route back through the interface it learned it from.
Honeypot & Honeynet
Honeypot
A deliberately attractive system (bait) designed to lure attackers. When an attacker accesses it, data is collected about their methods and techniques so weaknesses can be fixed. Must be an isolated system to keep real data safe.
Honeynet
A collection of honeypots forming a fake network designed to attract and trap attackers.
Padded Cell
A system set up to wait for an IDS to detect attackers and automatically transfer them to an isolated honeypot environment.
Honeypot for Email
Used to collect and analyze spam — the honeypot email address receives spam, helping identify spam sources and patterns.
Perimeter Networks (DMZ)

A DMZ (Demilitarized Zone) — also called a perimeter network — sits between the public internet and the private corporate network. Contains publicly accessible servers: web servers, email servers, proxy servers. Keeps public-facing services separated from the internal network.

Dual-Homed DMZ
Has firewalls inside AND outside the perimeter network. Two separate firewalls — provides stronger isolation. Best practice for high-security environments.
Single-Homed DMZ
One firewall connects all three network zones (internet, DMZ, internal). Simpler but less secure. Single point of failure.
Server & Domain Isolation / VLANs / Air Gap / DirectAccess
Server & Domain Isolation
Segmenting servers onto different subnets. In Windows domain: IPsec authenticates computers on different networks, allowing them to communicate while remaining isolated. Devices that should be on separate subnets: servers, printers, IoT devices.
VLAN (Virtual LAN)
A logical network built on a physical switch. A single 48-port switch can be configured for 16 admin ports, 16 sales ports, 16 operations ports. Separates network traffic logically without separate physical hardware. Administrators configure VLANs on managed switches.
Air Gap Network
Physical isolation — devices are completely disconnected from any network. No Wi-Fi, no ethernet, no connections. The most secure isolation. Used for: classified government systems, nuclear plant controls, critical infrastructure. Example: a computer that has never been connected to any network.
DirectAccess
Also known as Unified Remote Access. Provides domain-joined client computers secure remote access to domain resources WITHOUT using a VPN. Always-on — clients automatically connect to the corporate network when online. Opposite of isolation — it connects, not separates.

Network Address Translation (NAT / PAT)

Objective 3.3.d

Private IP addresses (192.168.x.x, 10.x.x.x, 172.16–31.x.x) cannot be routed on the public internet. NAT translates private IPs to public IPs for outbound traffic, and back for inbound response traffic.

Static NAT
One-to-one mapping: one private IP → one public IP. Rarely used — requires as many public IPs as private devices. Expensive.
Dynamic NAT (NAT Pool)
A pool of public IPs shared among private devices. Devices get a public IP from the pool when needed. Disadvantage: if all pool IPs are in use, new requests are blocked.
PAT (Port NAT) — Most Common
Multiple private IPs share a single public IP, differentiated by port numbers. Process: (1) Private IP request → assigned a port from the public IP. (2) Request sent to destination through the public IP. (3) Router maps the port back to the originating private IP. (4) Data returned to original private IP.

Protocol Security

Objectives 3.4.a through 3.4.c
Protocol Spoofing
Spoofing TypeHow It Works
DNS SpoofingRedirects a web request to an incorrect/imposter website. The attacker injects false DNS records. User believes they're on the real site.
ARP SpoofingAttacks IP packets until the source IP address is forged. Steals a host's IP address, redirecting traffic to the attacking machine. Enables MITM attacks on local networks.

IPsec Components
AH (Authentication Header)
Provides authentication, integrity, and anti-replay protection for data packets. Does NOT encrypt data — so it does not provide confidentiality. Prevents packet tampering and replay attacks.
ESP (Encapsulating Security Payload)
Provides full CIA (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability) for the IP payload. Encrypts the data payload. The "rule sheet" for the VPN connection structure. More complete than AH.
IKE (Internet Key Exchange)
Defines the method for the initial encryption key exchange between endpoints. Establishes the Security Association (SA). Uses certificates or pre-shared keys for authentication. IKEv2 is the newer version.
IPsec Modes
Transport Mode
Used for end-to-end communication (host to host). Encrypts only the data payload, not the IP header. Used for remote access and direct host-to-host connections.
Tunnel Mode
Used for server-to-server and server-to-gateway communication. Encrypts the entire original IP packet and encapsulates it in a new IP packet. Used for VPN tunnels between networks.
DNSSEC & Tunneling

DNSSEC (DNS Security Extensions) ensures outgoing internet traffic always reaches the correct server. Prevents DNS spoofing by authenticating and checking integrity of DNS responses via public key encryption. Implemented by enabling on a DNS server and limiting who can add DNS entries.

Tunneling creates a secure connection between two devices to protect data in transit. Three common tunnel protocols: L2TP, PPTP, SSTP (plus IKEv2). A tunnel encapsulates one network protocol within another.

Network Sniffing

Network sniffers (packet analyzers) capture and analyze network packets on connected ports. Used by administrators for troubleshooting and security analysis. Example: Wireshark. Can identify: bandwidth hogs, malformed packets, unusual traffic patterns, potential DoS activity. Attackers can also use sniffers — so encrypt sensitive traffic (HTTPS, VPN). Requires permission to use on a network you don't own.

Domain 4

Secure Computing

Email protection strategies, browser security management, and antimalware software lifecycle management.

Email Protection

Objectives 4.1.a through 4.1.e
Spoofing
Email sent that looks valid but is actually from an illegitimate email address — forging the "From" field. Used to trick recipients into trusting the email. Prevention: SPF records verify sender domains.
Phishing
A social engineering tactic using email to trick users into providing personal information — either by responding to the email directly or by clicking a link to a fake website. Prevention: SmartScreen Filter, user training, email filtering.
Pharming
Redirects a website's traffic to an illegitimate look-alike website (usually via DNS poisoning). The user types the correct URL but lands on the attacker's fake site. Prevention: DNSSEC, HTTPS verification.
Antispam
Bayesian filters are algorithms (stored in a database) that learn to identify spam based on email content patterns. Three antispam solutions: Bayesian filtering, blacklisting known spam IP addresses, and email gateway appliances/services. Most antispam settings operate at the server level.
SPF & PTR Records
SPF Record
Sender Policy Framework — an email validation system that lists approved IP addresses that can send email for your domain. Email from servers NOT on the SPF list is considered spam and blocked. Prevents source address spoofing. Published as a DNS TXT record.
PTR Record (Pointer Record)
Used for reverse DNS lookup — resolving an IP address to a hostname (opposite of normal DNS). When PTR records don't match domain records, email is flagged as spam. PTR records are created in the reverse DNS zone (not SMTP area). IP address is required when setting up a PTR record. Only email servers should have port 25 (SMTP) enabled — if a non-email server has SMTP enabled, it can be used to send spam.
DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)
An email authentication method that uses digital signatures to verify that an email message was sent by an authorized server and was not altered in transit. The sending server signs emails with a private key; the receiving server verifies using a public key published in DNS.
DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication)
An email authentication protocol that builds on SPF and DKIM. It tells receiving email servers what to do when messages fail SPF/DKIM checks: none (monitor only), quarantine (spam folder), or reject (block entirely). Publishes policies as DNS TXT records.
User Training Principles (Email & Web)
Email rules: (1) Never open email attachments from unknown senders. (2) Verify URLs before clicking — hover to see actual destination. (3) Never provide personal/company information via email unless the site is verified reputable. Web rules: Only visit HTTPS websites for sensitive transactions. Sensitive personal info should only be transmitted over an encrypted connection (HTTPS). SmartScreen Filter in Edge/IE helps prevent phishing.

Browser Security Management

Objectives 4.2.a · 4.2.b · 4.2.c
Browser Cache Management
Cache stores copies of web pages to speed up repeat visits. You can clear: browsing history, cookies, cached images and files, download history, passwords, form data. If web pages are failing to display the latest content → clear the browser cache. Set Edge as default browser in Windows Settings → Apps → Default apps.
Private Browsing
Private/Incognito mode: content saved and pages browsed are NOT stored under History on the local device. Does NOT make browsing truly private — your ISP, employer, and websites you visit can still see your activity. Pages browsed in private mode are not stored under the user's history profile. Windows only saves browser history under the current user profile, not globally.

Antimalware & Antivirus Software

Objectives 4.3.a through 4.3.g
Installation
Many antivirus apps try to make configuration changes during installation. Install a new antivirus ONLY if you have another one that can be immediately activated. Do not leave a gap in protection. Check for configuration prompts carefully during setup.
Uninstallation
Can be done via: Control Panel → Programs and Features, or Settings → Apps. Only uninstall unnecessary or conflicting antimalware (two simultaneous AV programs can conflict).
Updates
Must continually update definition files (signature files) to detect new malware. Most antimalware apps check for definition updates automatically. Old definitions = unable to detect new threats. Update both the application AND its definitions.
Scheduled Scans
Configure regular automatic scans. Most apps offer Quick Scan (common infection locations), Full Scan (entire system), and Custom Scan options. Run scans during off-hours to minimize performance impact.
Remediation
Remediation actions when malware is found: quarantine (isolate the malware), delete (remove the threat), restore (recover cleaned files). Always disconnect from the network first if infection is suspected. Two areas to investigate alerts: Windows Security Center and the antimalware app's own interface.
Investigating Alerts
Signs of potential malware: unusual increase in: CPU usage events, network activity events, disk access events. A group of errors regarding device log files can indicate malware attack. Also watch for: unexpected program crashes, slow performance, unknown processes in Task Manager, suspicious network connections.

Domain 1CIA

What do the CIA triangle and non-repudiation each protect in a security program?

Confidentiality keeps data private, Integrity keeps it accurate and unaltered, Availability keeps it reachable for authorized users, and Non-repudiation proves who performed an action so they cannot deny it later.

Domain 1Risk

How do threat, risk, and vulnerability differ?

A threat is a possible danger, a vulnerability is a specific weakness that can be exploited, and risk is the likelihood and impact of that threat exploiting the weakness.

Domain 1Controls

What is the difference between administrative controls and technical controls?

Administrative controls are people and process driven, like policies, training, and procedures. Technical controls are enforced by systems, like firewalls, IDS, MFA, and encryption.

Domain 1Physical

What are the three layers of physical security in defense in depth, and where does a mantrap fit?

The layers are the external perimeter, internal perimeter, and secure internal areas. A mantrap is a high-security access control point used in secure internal areas to stop tailgating.

Domain 1Malware

How would you distinguish a virus, worm, and Trojan horse on an exam question?

A virus needs a host or carrier, a worm self-replicates across networks without a host, and a Trojan horse pretends to be legitimate software while hiding malicious behavior.

Domain 1Advanced Malware

Why are rootkits, backdoors, and zero day attacks especially dangerous?

Rootkits hide with high privilege, backdoors create persistent unauthorized access, and zero day attacks exploit vulnerabilities before a patch exists.

Domain 1DoS

What is the difference between DoS and DDoS, and what do botnet and zombie mean?

DoS is a single-source flood attack, while DDoS uses many systems at once. A botnet is the attack network, and each compromised member device is a zombie.

Domain 1Attacks

Which common attack methods target identity, sessions, or web input?

IP spoofing forges a trusted source, replay reuses captured valid data, MITM intercepts traffic, XSS injects client-side scripts, and SQL injection abuses unsanitized database input.

Domain 1Backups

When would you choose full, incremental, or differential backups?

Full backups copy everything, incremental backups copy changes since the last backup of any type, and differential backups copy changes since the last full backup. Incrementals save space; differentials simplify restores.

Domain 2Hardening

What does hardening actually mean for a client or server?

Hardening means reducing the attack surface by disabling unneeded services, removing unused accounts and software, applying patches, separating services, and enforcing secure baseline settings.

Domain 2MFA

Why is username plus password not true MFA?

Because both are the same factor category: what you know. True MFA requires factors from different categories, such as password plus smart card or password plus fingerprint.

Domain 2Passwords

Which password policy settings matter most against brute force and password reuse?

Use a strong minimum length, require complexity, keep a password history, set a reasonable maximum age, and configure account lockout after repeated failed attempts.

Domain 2Active Directory

What does Active Directory do, and where do LDAP and Kerberos fit?

Active Directory centrally stores and manages users, groups, computers, and policies. LDAP is used to query and modify directory data, while Kerberos is the default authentication protocol that enables ticket-based SSO.

Domain 2Permissions

What is the practical difference between NTFS Read, Write, Modify, and Full Control?

Read views data, Write creates or changes data, Modify includes read, write, execute, and delete, and Full Control adds the ability to change permissions and take ownership. Deny overrides allows.

Domain 2Shares

How do share permissions differ from NTFS permissions?

Share permissions apply only over the network, while NTFS permissions apply on the local file system as well. Effective access is shaped by both, and best practice is to assign rights to groups rather than directly to users.

Domain 2Auditing

Why is auditing important, and where would you look on Windows?

Auditing supports accountability and non-repudiation by recording who did what and when. On Windows, you usually review the Security log in Event Viewer.

Domain 2Encryption

How do BitLocker, BitLocker To Go, and EFS differ?

BitLocker encrypts full internal drives, BitLocker To Go encrypts removable drives, and EFS encrypts individual files and folders on NTFS. TPM commonly supports BitLocker key protection.

Domain 2Crypto Basics

When should you think symmetric, asymmetric, hash, and digital signature?

Symmetric encryption uses one shared key and is fast, asymmetric encryption uses public and private keys for secure exchange, hashing is one-way for integrity, and a digital signature proves authenticity and detects tampering.

Domain 3Wireless

How would you rank WEP, WPA, and WPA2 on a security question?

WEP is broken and should never be used, WPA improved on WEP with TKIP but is outdated, and WPA2 with AES is the preferred standard in this guide.

Domain 3Wireless Setup

What quick wireless-hardening steps matter on a default access point?

Change default credentials, enable strong encryption, review the SSID configuration, and understand that MAC filtering can help but is not strong security by itself because MAC addresses can be spoofed.

Domain 3Firewalls

What is the difference between stateless and stateful firewall inspection?

Stateless inspection evaluates each packet independently, while stateful inspection tracks session state and understands whether return traffic belongs to a valid established conversation.

Domain 3Detection

How do IDS, IPS, and SIEM differ in day-to-day use?

An IDS detects and alerts, an IPS detects and actively blocks, and a SIEM aggregates security data and logs to correlate events, report, and sometimes automate response.

Domain 3DMZ

Why do organizations place public-facing systems in a DMZ?

A DMZ is a perimeter network between the internet and the private LAN. It isolates public-facing systems so compromise of a web or mail server does not immediately expose the internal network.

Domain 3NAT

How do static NAT, dynamic NAT, and PAT compare?

Static NAT is a fixed one-to-one mapping, dynamic NAT pulls from a public address pool, and PAT lets many private hosts share one public IP by translating ports.

Domain 3IPsec

What roles do AH, ESP, IKE, transport mode, and tunnel mode play in IPsec?

AH gives integrity and authentication, ESP adds encryption, IKE handles key exchange, transport mode protects host-to-host payloads, and tunnel mode protects the full packet in gateway or server tunnels.

Domain 3Protocols

Why do DNSSEC and encrypted tunneling matter on modern networks?

DNSSEC helps verify DNS answers so users reach legitimate destinations, while secure tunneling and encrypted protocols protect data from sniffing and tampering while it crosses untrusted networks.

Domain 3Ports

Which well-known ports should you be ready to recognize instantly?

Know at least these: 22 SSH, 23 Telnet, 25 SMTP, 53 DNS, 80 HTTP, 110 POP3, 143 IMAP, 389 LDAP, 443 HTTPS, and 636 LDAPS.

Domain 4Email

What defenses belong in an email-protection answer?

Good answers include Bayesian filtering, user awareness, anti-spam tooling, restricting SMTP port 25 to mail servers, and DNS-based protections like SPF and PTR records.

Domain 4Email Auth

How do SPF, DKIM, and DMARC work together?

SPF says which servers may send mail for a domain, DKIM signs the message to prove integrity and origin, and DMARC tells receivers what to do if SPF or DKIM validation fails.

Domain 4Awareness

What user behaviors matter most for phishing, pharming, and social-media safety?

Users should verify links before clicking, avoid unexpected attachments, confirm HTTPS and certificate legitimacy for sensitive tasks, and never share company information publicly without approval.

Domain 4Browser

What does clearing cache fix, and what does private browsing not hide?

Clearing cache helps when pages are stale or behaving oddly. Private browsing keeps history and local traces off the device profile, but it does not hide activity from employers, ISPs, or the sites being visited.

Domain 4Cookies

Why are cookies not the same thing as spyware?

Cookies are legitimate text files used for site state and preferences. Spyware secretly collects information or monitors behavior without consent, making it malware rather than a normal browser feature.

Domain 4Definitions

Why are definition files so central to antivirus and antimalware?

Definition or signature files let security tools recognize known threats. If definitions are outdated, the tool may miss current malware even if the product itself is installed correctly.

Domain 4Remediation

What are the typical remediation options when malware is found?

The usual actions are quarantine, delete, or restore after cleanup. If infection is suspected, disconnect from the network first so the threat cannot spread or phone home.

Domain 4Indicators

Which symptoms often suggest a malware infection or active compromise?

Watch for spikes in CPU, disk, or network activity, suspicious log errors, unknown processes, crashes, or sudden slowness. These are classic reasons to investigate further.

Domain 4Secure Computing

What should users look for before entering sensitive information on a website?

They should confirm the site uses HTTPS, verify the domain is correct, check the certificate if needed, and rely on browser protections like SmartScreen to help flag suspicious pages and downloads.

Complete Glossary

All key terms defined — organized alphabetically for rapid reference.

Key Terms — Full Definitions

3DES
Triple DES — a block cipher that applies DES encryption three times. Less vulnerable than DES. Slower than AES.
Access Control
The act of restricting both physical and file/server access so those who need it have it, but those who do not need it do not have it.
Account Lockout
The process of preventing an account from logging on after several incorrect logon attempts. A key defense against brute force attacks.
Active Directory
Windows domain service storing and administering users, groups, printers, devices. Uses LDAP, Kerberos. Enables SSO.
Administrative Share
A type of share only accessible by administrators, backup operators, and server operators. Hidden shares end with a $ sign (e.g., C$, ADMIN$).
Adware
Malware that displays ads inside programs without user consent. Often bundled with free software.
AES
Advanced Encryption Standard. Block cipher available in 128, 192, and 256-bit keys. Most secure symmetric encryption algorithm. Government standard.
AH (IPsec)
Authentication Header — provides authentication, integrity, and anti-replay for packets. Does NOT encrypt payload.
Antispam
Software used to prevent spam email from reaching a client's email program. Uses Bayesian filters, blacklisting, and email gateway appliances.
Antivirus / Antimalware
Software that helps to ward off malware. Windows Defender is the built-in Windows antimalware app. Requires constantly updated definition files.
Application-Level Firewall
A firewall that does filtering at the application layer of the OSI model (Layer 7); also known as a proxy server. 3rd generation firewall.
ARP Spoofing
Compromising MAC address/IP address combinations through stealing the IP of a host, then forcing traffic to an attacking machine. Enables MITM on LANs.
Asymmetric Encryption
Uses two keys: public key to encrypt, private key to decrypt. Solves key distribution problem. Slower than symmetric.
Attack Surface
Total of all ways a system can be attacked — all apps, services, open ports, and connected devices.
Auditing
Tracking user activity — who logged in, what was accessed, when. Enables non-repudiation.
Availability
The part of the CIA triangle that ensures data is accessible by those who need it when they need it. Protected by redundancy, failover, and DDoS mitigation.
Authentication
The act of verifying identification to an application or a system. Can use passwords, tokens, biometrics, or certificates.
Backdoor
Unauthorized access point in software/hardware, either created by malware or left by developers. Enables persistent remote access.
Base-64-Encoded X.509
A certificate type that supports the storage of a single certificate, but not a private key. Text-based encoding format (.cer).
Biometrics
Authentication using unique physical characteristics: fingerprint, retina, iris, voice, face. "Who you are" factor.
BitLocker
Windows full-disk encryption. Uses TPM if available. Encrypts entire internal drives.
BitLocker To Go
Windows encryption for removable drives (USB, external HDD). Drive requires password/key to access on other machines.
Block Cipher
Encrypts data in fixed-size blocks using a key. Examples: AES, DES, 3DES.
Botnet
Network of compromised computers used to perform DDoS attacks. Controlled by attacker via C2 servers. Individual machines = zombies.
Brute Force Attack
Systematically trying every possible password combination using automated tools.
Buffer Overflow
Writing more data to a buffer than it can hold, overflowing into adjacent memory. Allows arbitrary code execution.
CA (Certificate Authority)
A server or third-party entity used to issue digital certificates. Enterprise CA uses Active Directory and auto-enrolls. Stand-alone CA does not.
CHAP
Challenge Handshake Authentication Protocol. Uses MD5 hashing. Never sends password directly. VPN authentication form.
CIA Triangle
Confidentiality + Integrity + Availability. The foundational model for all information security decisions.
Circuit Filtering
A firewall filtering method that analyzes data at Layer 5 (the Session layer) of the OSI model. 2nd generation firewall type.
Confidentiality
The act of keeping data and systems secure from unauthorized access. Part of the CIA triangle.
Cookie
A piece of text that stores information from a webpage, such as user information, site preferences, and shopping cart contents. NOT malware — cookies are legitimate text files, unlike spyware.
CRL
Certificate Revocation List. Stores revoked certificates. CA must update immediately when a certificate is compromised.
Cross-Site Scripting (XSS)
An injection of a script that bypasses a web browser's security mechanism. Targets client-side scripts. Mitigated by input validation.
DDoS Attack
Distributed Denial of Service — a DoS attack using multiple machines (botnet). Much harder to defend than single-source DoS.
Delegation
The act of passing control of a resource (such as an organizational unit) from one entity to another. Done via the Delegation of Control Wizard.
DER X.509
Distinguished Encoding Rules — binary encoded X.509 certificates that do NOT support private key storage.
DES
Data Encryption Standard — a 56-bit block cipher type of encryption. Considered weak and outdated. Replaced by 3DES and AES.
Defense in Depth
Multiple overlapping layers of security. If one layer fails, others remain. Both physical and logical security concept.
Device Guard
Windows tool using code integrity policies to allow only trusted apps to run. Whitelist-based lockdown.
Dictionary Attack
An attack in which a list of potential passwords (common words, phrases) is used to try to guess a password. Faster but less thorough than brute force.
Digital Certificate
Stores and shares public keys plus identity info (user, org, serial number, expiration). Issued by CAs.
Digital Signature
Verifies authenticity of a document or email. Uses sender's private key to sign; verified with sender's public key.
DirectAccess
Unified Remote Access. Gives domain-joined clients automatic, VPN-less access to corporate resources. Always-on connection.
DMZ
Demilitarized Zone. Perimeter network between public internet and private network. Houses publicly accessible servers.
DNS Poisoning
An injection of a bogus destination for an IP address into a DNS cache. Redirects users to attacker-controlled sites.
DNS Spoofing
A redirection of a web request to an incorrect website via forged DNS records. User believes they're on the real site.
DNSSEC
DNS Security Extensions. Ensures internet traffic reaches correct servers via authentication with public key encryption. Prevents DNS spoofing.
DoS Attack
Denial of Service — an attack in which networks are disrupted to the point where they cannot function. Single machine floods a target.
Dynamic NAT
A form of NAT in which a private device gets a public IP address from a pool of available public IP addresses. Pool can run out.
EAP-MS-CHAPv2
Universal auth framework supporting biometrics and smart cards. The ONLY VPN auth form supporting "who you are" factor.
EFS
Encrypting File System. Windows NTFS-level encryption for files/folders. Cannot compress and encrypt simultaneously.
Effective Permissions
The cumulative total of inherited and explicit permissions given to a user or group on a resource. When in multiple groups, permissions are combined (most permissive) unless Deny is applied.
Email Bombing
A DoS attack in which massive volumes of email are sent to a single address to overwhelm the mail server.
Encryption
The adding of ciphertext to data to scramble it and make it unreadable without a decryption key. Protects data at rest and in transit.
Enterprise Root CA
A certificate authority that sits at the top of a hierarchy of certificate authorities. Uses Active Directory and auto-enrolls users.
ESP (IPsec)
Encapsulating Security Payload. Provides full CIA for the IP payload. Encrypts data. Most complete IPsec component.
Event Viewer
A Windows tool used to store and present application, security, and system logs for troubleshooting and auditing. Audit results found under Windows Logs → Security.
Explicit Permissions
Permissions that are granted to a user or group directly by an administrator, as opposed to inherited permissions from parent folders.
External Perimeter
The entry area to a building and the immediate area outside of a building. Secured by fences, bollards, lighting, cameras, badge readers, and guards.
Firewall
Network security system controlling inbound/outbound traffic based on admin-defined rules. Can be hardware or software.
Group Policy
A Windows tool that is used to control rights for users, groups, and organizational units. Updates push approximately every 90 minutes by default.
Hardening
Reducing attack surface: disable unused services, remove unnecessary accounts, apply patches, rename admin accounts.
Hash Function
A one-way encryption type that offers no decryption. Used to protect passwords (MD5, SHA-1, SHA-256). If two inputs produce the same hash (collision), the function is compromised.
Hoax (Virus Hoax)
A fake virus often used to detract attention away from a real virus. Looks real but isn't — designed to cause panic or waste resources.
Honeynet
Multiple honeypots forming a decoy network to trap attackers. Collects attack data for analysis.
Honeypot
Deliberately vulnerable decoy system that attracts attackers. Isolated from real network. Collects attacker intelligence.
IDS (Intrusion Detection System)
Monitors network traffic and alerts when suspicious activity is detected. Passive — detects and reports but does NOT automatically stop attacks. Uses signature files.
IKE / IKEv2
Internet Key Exchange. Defines initial encryption key exchange method between VPN endpoints. IKEv2 supports auto-reconnect.
Inherited Permissions
Permissions on a file or folder that are present because of permissions given to a parent folder or drive. Shown as gray check marks in the UI.
Integrity
The part of the CIA triangle that ensures data is accurate, valid, and protected against unauthorized changes — whether stored or in transit.
Internal Perimeter
The area of a building immediately inside of the entryway. Secured by locked interior doors, reception desks, visitor badges, and alarm systems.
IP Address Spoofing
An attack where a source IP address is forged in packet headers to impersonate a trusted source or hide the attacker's identity.
IPS (Intrusion Prevention System)
Monitors traffic AND actively blocks or shuts down suspicious activity. Active — detects AND prevents. An IDS with automated response capability.
IPsec
Suite of protocols (AH, ESP, IKE) protecting data in transit. Used by L2TP and IKEv2 VPN tunnels.
Kerberos
Default Windows network authentication protocol. Ticket-based. Used by Active Directory to authenticate domain users.
Keylogger
Hardware or software that records keystrokes. Hardware = not detectable by AV. Software = detectable by AV.
L2TP
Layer 2 Tunneling Protocol. Industry-standard VPN tunnel protocol. Uses IPsec for encryption. More secure than PPTP.
LDAP
Lightweight Directory Access Protocol. Used to query and modify Active Directory data. Port 389. Secured: LDAPS port 636.
MAC Filter
Limits which hardware MAC addresses can connect to a wireless access point. Easily bypassed by spoofing — not a primary security measure.
Malicious Software Removal Tool
A Windows tool used to remove malware from a system. Supplements antivirus but does not replace it.
Logic Bomb
Malicious code triggered by a specific event or date. Lies dormant until triggered. Often planted by disgruntled insiders.
Malware
Malicious software — any file, program, or code used to cause harm to a system. Includes viruses, worms, Trojans, spyware, etc.
Mantrap
A physical security gateway with two interlocked doors — only one opens at a time. Captures tailgaters. Used in high-security physical access control.
Man in the Middle (MITM)
An interception of data being transferred. Data can be captured and/or manipulated on the way to a destination. Prevented by encryption.
Mantrap
Two interlocked doors — only one opens at a time. Captures tailgaters. Used in high-security physical access control.
MS-CHAPv2
Microsoft CHAP Version 2. Two-way (mutual) authentication — stronger than CHAP. Most common VPN auth protocol.
MFA
Multifactor Authentication. Requires two or more factors from DIFFERENT categories (know, have, are).
NAT
Network Address Translation. Hides private IPs behind public IPs. Three types: Static, Dynamic (pool), PAT (most common).
Network Sniffer
A tool used to capture network packets on connected ports. Can be used for analysis or hacking. Example: Wireshark. Requires permission to use.
Non-Repudiation
The ability to prove a specific entity performed a specific action, preventing denial. Achieved through digital signatures, audit logs, and timestamps.
Offline Files
Copies of files from network drives that allow a user to work on files even when not connected to the network. Managed through Sync Center in Control Panel.
Packet Filtering
A filtering mechanism in which data packets are filtered by port and/or protocol. 1st generation firewall type. Layer 3 (Network) of the OSI model.
PAP
Password Authentication Protocol. Sends password in plain text. NEVER use on secure networks. Completely insecure.
Password History
A list of passwords for a user that, when set, cannot be reused until a specified number of different passwords have been used. Prevents password recycling.
Permission Level
A specific permission that can be granted on files and/or folders. NTFS levels: Read, Read & Execute, List Folder Contents, Write, Modify, Full Control.
PGP
Pretty Good Privacy. Email encryption using public + session key to encrypt; private key + session key to decrypt.
Pharming
Redirects a website's legitimate traffic to a malicious look-alike site. Often via DNS poisoning.
Phishing
Email-based social engineering to steal personal info or credentials via fake emails or fraudulent websites.
PKCS #7
Personal Information Exchange Format #7. A cryptographic message syntax standard that supports the storage of certificates but NOT private keys.
PKCS #12
Personal Information Exchange Format #12. Supports the storage and exporting of BOTH certificates AND private keys. The ONLY format that stores both (.pfx/.p12).
PKI
Public Key Infrastructure. System for creating, managing, distributing, using, storing, and revoking digital certificates.
Polymorphic Virus
A virus that constantly morphs (changes its code signature) so that antivirus software has a tough time catching it. Also called metamorphic.
PPTP
Point-to-Point Tunneling Protocol. Old, weak VPN tunnel protocol. Not recommended. Use L2TP/IPsec or IKEv2 instead.
Principle of Least Privilege
Users receive only the minimum access needed for their job. Reduces attack surface and limits damage from compromised accounts.
Private Key
A type of key used to decrypt data in asymmetric encryption. Kept secret by the owner. Used at the end of data transmission.
Protocol Spoofing
An imposter-like misuse of a network protocol to attack a network. Types: DNS spoofing, ARP spoofing, IP spoofing.
PTR Record
Pointer Record. Used for reverse DNS lookup — resolving an IP address to a hostname (opposite of normal DNS). When PTR doesn't match, email is flagged as spam.
Public Key
A shared key used to start asymmetric encryption. Used at the beginning of data transmission to encrypt data sent to the key owner.
RADIUS
Remote Authentication Dial-In User Service. Authenticates external connections from dial-ups, VPNs, WAPs. Common for wireless auth.
Ransomware
Malware that blocks (locker) or encrypts (crypto) data until ransom is paid. Notable: WannaCry, CryptoLocker, Petya.
RC4
Popular stream cipher encryption algorithm. Performs bit-by-bit encryption. Faster than block ciphers but considered weak.
Registry
Windows central database storing all configuration info. Five hives: HKLM, HKCU, HKU, HKCR, HKCC. A DWORD is a 32-bit data value.
Removable Device
A device easily added/removed from a computer: DVDs, thumb drives, external drives, memory cards. Should be encrypted with BitLocker To Go.
Replay Attack
Captured valid network data is re-transmitted to impersonate a legitimate user. Prevented by timestamps and nonces.
Residual Risk
The remaining amount of risk after mitigation takes place. Can never be fully eliminated — only reduced to acceptable levels.
Risk
The probability that a threat will become a reality. Calculated as: Risk = Threat × Vulnerability. Managed through acceptance, avoidance, mitigation, or transfer.
Risk Acceptance
Acknowledging a risk exists but taking no action. Used when cost of mitigation exceeds the potential impact.
Risk Avoidance
The act of doing nothing about a risk — choosing to avoid the activity that creates the risk entirely.
Risk Mitigation
The act of lessening a risk and/or the impact of a risk through countermeasures and controls.
Risk Transfer
The sharing of a risk burden — typically through insurance or outsourcing to a third party.
RODC
Read-Only Domain Controller. Holds read-only copy of AD. Cannot be modified locally. Ideal for remote/branch offices.
Rootkit
Malware that gains admin-level access and masks itself from detection. Often affects BIOS or firmware. Very difficult to remove.
Routing
The connecting of networks. A routing table is used to decide where packets go from one network to the next.
Run As
A Windows feature that allows a user to run a program using a different account (like an administrator account) without logging out.
S/MIME
Secure/Multipurpose Internet Mail Extension. Used by email providers and web browsers for encryption and embedding objects in email.
SCCM
System Center Configuration Manager. Used to push out updates to client machines. Enterprise-level alternative to WSUS.
Secure Areas
Areas of a building that should have a restricted list of people who can enter them — server rooms, data centers, executive offices.
Secure Dynamic DNS Updates
A DNS setting where only members of an Active Directory domain can create records on a DNS server. Prevents DNS poisoning.
Security Baseline
A collection of settings used to provide a positive security impact. A known-good configuration state for a system.
Secure Website
A website which starts with an HTTPS prefix and shows a lock (padlock) icon in the browser. Clicking the lock shows the site's digital certificate.
Security Compliance Manager
A tool for security baseline management features and to export security baselines for deployment.
Separation of Services
A concept by which multiple services are installed across multiple servers rather than one. Reduces attack surface per server.
Shares
Permissions granted on files and folders over the network. Permission levels: Full Control, Change, and Read. Do NOT apply when logged in locally.
Software Vulnerability Attack
An attack in which a virus or worm takes advantage of a software vulnerability — an unpatched bug or flaw in code. Patching is the primary defense.
SIEM
Security Information & Event Manager. Collects security data, creates reports, automates responses. Example: Azure Sentinel.
Smart Card
A badge-like device waved, swiped, or inserted into a device for authentication. "What you have" factor.
SmartScreen Filter
A tool used with Edge and IE to help prevent users from falling victim to phishing and malicious downloads.
Social Engineering
Tricking people into revealing sensitive information by impersonating a legitimate entity. Forms include phishing, vishing, pretexting.
SPF
Sender Policy Framework. Email validation DNS record listing approved sending IP addresses. Prevents spoofing.
Spyware
Covertly collects personal info without user knowledge. Sends data to third parties. Different from cookies (which are legitimate).
SQL Injection
An attack using SQL code to gain access to a SQL database and run malicious code. Prevented by input validation and parameterized queries.
SSID
Service Set Identifier. The name of a wireless network broadcast by a WAP. Can be hidden (disabled broadcast).
SSL
Secure Socket Layer. Uses asymmetric encryption, primarily on websites and VPN connections. Predecessor to TLS.
SSO
Single Sign-On. An authentication mechanism that allows a single sign-on account to access multiple resources without re-authenticating.
SSTP
Secure Socket Tunneling Protocol. VPN tunnel using HTTPS over TCP port 443. Passes most firewalls easily.
Stand-Alone CA
A CA that does not use Active Directory and does not automatically enroll people with certificates. Manual enrollment only.
Stateful Firewall Inspection
Inspects data based on source/destination IP, packet type, and port number. Session state IS stored, and return traffic IS allowed.
Stateless Firewall Inspection
Inspects data based on source/destination IP, packet type, and port number. Session state is NOT stored — each packet treated independently.
Static NAT
A form of NAT which maps one private IP address to one public IP address. Permanent 1:1 mapping.
Stream Cipher
A type of encryption that performs bit-by-bit encryption. Faster than block ciphers. Example: RC4.
Strong Password
At least 8 characters with at least 3 of 4 types: uppercase, lowercase, numbers, symbols. Changed regularly.
Subnetting
The use of logical networks to segment devices according to machine type and security needs. Limits broadcast domains.
Symmetric Encryption
Same key encrypts AND decrypts. Faster than asymmetric. Challenge: securely sharing the key. Examples: AES, DES, RC4.
Syslog
Industry-standard logging system used to audit non-Microsoft products and network devices.
Threat
A possibility of data or systems being compromised. Differs from risk (the probability a threat becomes reality).
TLS
Transport Layer Security. Successor to SSL. Encrypts data in transit. Used for HTTPS (port 443) and secure communications.
Token Device
A small device with a form of electronic key used as a "what you have" authentication factor. Generates one-time codes.
TPM
Trusted Platform Module. Hardware chip for cryptographic keys. Used by BitLocker and virtual smart cards.
Threat Modeling
Process of identifying threats and vulnerabilities, then defining countermeasures to prevent them.
Transport Mode (IPsec)
A mode of IPsec used for end-to-end communications between two hosts. Only payload is encrypted.
Trojan Horse
Imposter program appearing legitimate but containing hidden malware. Does not self-replicate. Installed by tricking users.
Tunnel Mode (IPsec)
A mode of IPsec used for server-to-server and server-to-gateway communications. Entire packet is encrypted.
Tunneling
A mechanism used to transmit data over part of a VPN connection securely by encapsulating packets within other packets.
UAC
User Account Control. Prompts for confirmation when apps make system changes. Has 4 settings; default is recommended.
Unsecure Auth Protocols
Protocols that transfer authentication data in plain text: PAP, FTP, and Telnet. Use SSH instead of Telnet. Use SFTP instead of FTP.
Virtual Smart Card
A type of smart card that is not physical but uses the TPM chip on a computer instead of a separate card reader.
VLAN
Virtual LAN. Logical network on a physical switch. Separates network segments without separate hardware.
VPN
Virtual Private Network. Encrypted private tunnel over public network. Secures remote connections to corporate network.
Virus
Malicious code that requires a host/carrier. Spreads when infected file is executed. Types: file infector, macro, boot sector.
Vulnerability
A weakness in the Confidentiality, Integrity, or Availability of data. A specific exploitable weakness (vs. a general threat).
WEP
Wired Equivalent Privacy. Oldest wireless encryption. CRACKED. NEVER use WEP.
WPA
Wi-Fi Protected Access. Uses TKIP (Temporal Key Integrity Protocol) and a shared key to secure wireless networks. Better than WEP but outdated.
Windows Defender
An antimalware app that ships with Windows. Provides real-time protection. Definitions must be constantly updated.
Windows Firewall
The built-in Windows software firewall. Uses rules to allow or block incoming or outgoing traffic. Managed through Control Panel or GPO.
WPA / WPA2
WPA: uses TKIP (legacy). WPA2: uses AES + IEEE 802.11i (current standard). WPA2 is always preferred. WPA3 is newest.
WSUS
Windows Server Update Services. Used to push Windows updates to client machines from a central server.
Worm
Self-replicating malware that spreads without a host. Doesn't destroy data but consumes CPU, bandwidth, memory.
Zero Day Attack
Exploits an unknown vulnerability before the vendor can release a patch. No existing defense. Keep systems patched always.
Zombie
A computer infected and controlled by an attacker as part of a botnet. Carries out DDoS attacks without owner's knowledge.
